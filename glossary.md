## Geomatics

Geomatics is the term used for the study of the data and recollection of the physical impact humans and other creatures and events have on the 
closest layer of the earth. Composing studies about geography using state of the art technologies and techniques. The term was first proposed
in french by a scientist called Bernard Dubuison at the end of the 1960's mainly to reflect on changes from other jobs like surveyor or 
photogrammetrist. At the end of the 20th century it became clear that a change was needed in order to prepare for the upcoming changes to update
the gear and tactics used to record and study geographic data by merging traditional disciplines of land surveying with emerging tools for data 
capture, manipulation, storage and diffusion. In truth Geomatics is really just an umbrella term that talks about the tools and techniques used 
to analyse the Earth’s surface.[^1]

This term is used for many fields that are used in everyday life. examples like cartography, GPS services, Statistics, environmental consulting 
and many other incredibly useful domains profit from geomatics. In Canada its the Canada Center for Mapping and Earth Observation (CCMEO) is the 
institute in charge of geomatics, mapping and earth observations in Canada.[^2] Fields like land surveying are mainly used to help establish land 
boundaries that cultivate into current, legal property. Another great field that benefits from geomatics its geovisualization which is a study that 
merges computer sciences and cartography by creating hyper realistic 3D height maps of land areas.[^3] Another field that greatly benefits from this 
term is hydrogeomatics where by using emerging technologies we are able to visualize all that is above and under sea level in hopes of gathering 
useful data.

In conclusion geomatics is the term of updating pre-existing fields with new technologies and techniques to be able to gather new data that is 
useful for the modern age. Geomatics a very useful and needed domain that lets us get to know our world in a much more precise way that lets us 
take care of it in a much more effective way.

## Biomachine
Biomachine seems to go down two different routes, either a machine being integrated into a living creature,or a machine modeled after a mechanic of a creature. New media can be used to define these devices because of the idea of their purpose.
New media “cuts the middle man” of older technology by controlling everything in the background, by incorporating machines into biology[^BioDesign]:, this could make movement automatic, resulting in physical new media.
This idea can also be applied to the other definition, for example, Terragrippers. This device allows pinpoint medicine delivery to specific places in the intestine.
This action simplifies the once more manual task. Stanley Cohen and Herbert Boyer seem to be the pioneers of this term and field .[^BirthOfBio]. They first worked with ringlets in bacteria, experimenting with incorporating DNA that in turn would act as a “copier”.
This later branches into genetic engineering and other things, but they seemed to be the people who opened this “door”[^IntroBio]. Biomachines now seem to cover areas of major concern, medical, energy and lifeforms, but the possibilities seem endless. A popular understanding of a biomachine is a cyborg.
Though mostly coined in the movies, a cyborg is a person genetically enhanced by machine, this can now be seen in creatures like jellyfish and cockroaches. Simple lifeforms that’s tasks can be assisted in a simple fashion. Still a very scary thought.
Biomachinery seems to have only just begun and is at the cutting edge of technology, maybe one day being a common term.

## Pixel Flow vs Sweat Flow
---
Pixel Flow is a concept first coined and explained by writer Joanna Zylinska in her paper: BIO-AI: The Aesthetics and ethics of data animism.[^1]
It is expressing how can a machine *make* and syntesizes images. She thens put the term in contrast with the concept of sweat flow, defining 
the human tangible labour we go through in the analogous world.This contrast and opposition is exactly where both concepts find their meaning. It connects to the opposition of agency and autonomy as an 
HCI boundary.[^2] These concepts find their relevance in new media theory to better understand the constant evolving world we live in and regain power.
The distinction between how a machine can reach a flow state and how can humans reach this trans-like working mentality is where we can find
our true human agency. The world of AI is blurring this line and it can be scary to lose our sense of control or feel overwhelmed by the daily
evolution of machine models or agents. We can illsutrate this contrast of pixel vs sweat flow in an example: Pixel flow would define how
a machine, AI in this case, can create an image based on visual databases and sources. This autonomous work is the flow, the automatic 
synthesizing of visual representations. In opposition; sweat flow could be illustrated with a tile worker making a mosaic out of tangible
tiles and grout based on an image in his mind. This kind of work demands attention, care and **sweat** to execute work based on intentions and agency.
In a way, this is borrowing the concept of databases from Lee Manovich's *Database as a Symbolic Form*[^3] Because the machine or the human 
is basing his *flow* from a database, an organized information set, numerical or a human collection of found and made memories. 

**Therefore, pixel flow and sweat flow are 
opposing concepts that embodies how a human and a machine can work and think differently utilizing numerical autonomy and human agency to reach
a desirable goal.**

## Data Animism

Before delving into Data Animism, a term coined by Joanna Zylinska, it is important to first define animism itself. The concept was introduced by anthropologist Edward Burnett Tylor in 1871 and described as the attribution of spirit, life, agency, or sentience to nonhuman beings, such as animals, plants, objects or natural phenomena.[^1]

Zylinska works animism into the digital sphere by extending the attribution of sentience to technology, specifically artificial intelligence. Its ability to create, reproduce and make seemingly autonomous decisions gives it a sense of agency, encouraging us to view it as more than just a tool. AI also moves us physically and emotionally, serving as a biomachine capable of channeling life through data. In doing so, it shapes how we perceive, feel and engage with the world.[^2]

This term’s significance lies in the extent to which AI has taken root in the infrastructures of our daily lives, no longer serving as an external tool but as a fundamental presence. It reshapes social organization and existential concerns, taking our data (our language, images, and behaviors) and creating new meaning, producing a feedback loop that embeds it deeper into human experience. Data Animism explains how AI is no longer simply a technology we use, but now an active participant in reorganizing life.

This can also be seen in current media practices and technologies. Some major examples of this is include:
- Generative AI: Tools like ChatGPT, Gemini and Midjourney produce language and images that feel personal and intentional, all the while being entirely algorithmic.
- Voice Assistants: Virtual assistants like Alexa and Siri blur the boundaries between ‘man’ and ‘machine’, driving users to talk to them as if they were people.[^3]
- Early Cinema: Audiences gave life to the moving images on screen, for example recoiling from the Lumière brothers' 1895 projected train.[^2]

## **Interface Effect**

The *interface effect* is a term from Alexander R. Galloway’s book *The Interface Effect* (2012).[^1]
Instead of thinking of an interface like a website, app, or game screen; think of it as a neutral window 
we look through to access content, Galloway suggests that interfaces are guiding us in how we experience media.
In other words, they don’t just show us things, they shape what we see, how we act, and how we think about that media [^2]
Galloway’s point is that an interface isn’t just a tool. It’s a 'structure of possibilities and limits'.
It decides what is visible, what is clickable, what actions are easy or impossible, and even what feels “normal” or
expected when we use digital systems. Interfaces set the 'rules of engagement' without us necessarily noticing, and this 
is what he calls the *interface effect*, the influence the interface has on behavior and meaning.
For example, social media feeds, infinite scrolling, autoplay buttons, notifications, and recommended content don’t
just happen by accident. They are part of the interface design that guides your attention and decisions. The interface
makes some actions feel natural, like scrolling forever, and makes others harder, like finding your settings or deleting
an account. As one reviewer puts it, Galloway shows us that interfaces are cultural and political environments,not invisible 
backgrounds [^3].
This idea is significant in new media because it challenges the old assumption that interfaces are simple platforms. 
Instead, the interface is part of the *media experience itself* and shapes how digital culture works.

## Machine Learning

Machine learning is a process closely related to artificial intelligence, through which algorithms analyze their given training data and form the ability to make inferences about new data. As a result of this process, algorithms begin to recognize patterns (model training) and can subsequently make decisions that can result in the generation of original content. This process has been used as the backbone of various generative AI tools. The American computer scientist Arthur Samuel coined the term back in 1959.  [^bergmann25machinelearning]

There are many applications of machine learning, including scientific and medical research. It has also drastically reshaped the media landscape. Machine learning fuels social media algorithms, guiding what content each user can experience. Additionally, beginning in the 2020s, the content available online has changed as a result of an influx of AI-generated art, videos, music, and more. Many creators online condemned the use of AI in art, whereas many creators embraced AI to varying degrees in their works, whether they use it for idea production, as tools in their work, or as the final product of their project. The lack of transparency regarding the use of this technology has posed a huge problem in the media landscape, where people admonish its use in creative processes. On the other hand, generative AI has made art and creation much more accessible and allows established artists to work more efficiently and to broaden their scopes. [^valtonen2019medialiteracy]

Another issue is bias in generated content, where the model that harnesses machine learning can be unfairly influenced by its developers, its training data, etc. Furthermore, the illegal scraping of data has put major tech companies in hot water regarding their products. To this day, companies are attempting to eliminate bias and make their products more ethical, which is likely to be a problem that persists indefinitely. [^chang2024mediaresearch]

## Prompts

Prompts are natural language instructions that specify how to complete the task at hand.
This is almost always submitted by a real person to the machine, usually an artificial intelligence system which will then generate your desired output. This term is significant in new media as prompting could be viewed as a main way of having computer-human interactions within generative Ai, options like text to text, text to image, text to video, video to audio, image to video, etc. [^jingxuan23model]

Prompts are significant because it gives autonomy to the author to write a prompt never used before. Compared to early versions of generative Ai, users do not need to know how to code or do any fancy stuff. All it takes is a natural language to communicate which lowers the barrier to produce new media. Lev Manovich speaks about a similar principle about how digital objects are never fixed but exist as infinitely modifiable outputs shaped by the user's input. [^manovich01text]

The word prompt originates from the latin word promptus which means "to bring forward”. It started to be used in the English language during the mid 14th century indicating with a sense to “urge someone into action”. Then by the 15th century it was being used to describe a hint or suggestion. By the late 17th century it developed a theatrical meaning, a person who prompted an actor meant they were assisting them with forgotten lines. [^harper26last]

## Surveillance Technology

Surveillance technology is the technology, medium, and tools used to observe, track, analyze, and collect data. The surveillance can be done
on people, events, on the internet, and in public spaces. It is present in the physical and digital world. The technologies that are
crafted to do these tasks can take different forms, depending on the needs of the people, corporations, and authorities. For example, the 
tech component can be a tool to recognize face ID, a tool to recognize fingerprints, cameras, trackers, etc. It is seen in all 
kinds of environments, such as banks, airports, social media, and the internet. [^survTech]

When it comes to surveillance technology, there is a fine line on whether it is used in an ethical or non-ethical way. Ethical ways can
be when it is used to monitor employee screen activity during work, to monitor markets and finances, and to protect from fraudulent individuals
entering an establishment, to keep records of who enters and exits a space, to monitor activities in an airport, etc. It can be necessary for 
authorities to track criminals as well. Surveillance technology is a good practice when it's being used to enforce safety, to protect the population, 
and to track fraudulent activities and people. 

Although, there are rising concerns about how surveillance technology may be crossing the line because it is harming human rights. 
There were cases where the surveillance of data was taken too far; for example, surveillance technology was used to spy on political opponents 
and political activities, as mentioned on the ECCHR website. This is not a good motive for using this technology, as it can influence politcal 
outcomes unfairly. [^ECCHR] Also, corporations and governments do not disclose entirely how they use their technology to track data and monitor people. 

In the context of new media, surveillance technology is being used by social media platforms to track their users' behavior to be able 
to curate their algorithms. It is also used for safety and security with features such as facial recognition and authentication tools. 
But it is also discussed how they can take advantage and invade our privacy by collecting more data than needed. Social media collects
information regarding what their user is watching, sharing, liking, searching, etc. This alters a user's behavior and consumption 
towards that platform, as well as having impacts on their cognitive and human behaviors. [^UTS]

## Visibility

In the context of new media, visibility refers to the condition of being seen, recognized, and made present within digital networks. It is not only physical sight, but algorithmic exposure: the degree to which individuals, ideas, images, and data circulate across platforms such as social media, search engines, and streaming services. Visibility determines who is seen, heard, and which narratives gain legitimacy. In digital environments, to exist socially often means to be visible.

Historically, visibility has been tied to power. In earlier times, public visibility was associated with spectacle and surveillance, a dynamic theorized by Michel Foucault through the metaphor of the Panopticon[^michelf]. Later, new media expanded the scale of visibility, enabling centralized institutions to shape public perception. The general public now thus link visibility with representation: marginalized groups fought to be visible in mainstream media as a matter of political recognition.

Technologically, new media transforms visibility through digitization, data tracking, and algorithmic sorting. Surveillance capitalism monetizes visibility by turning user behavior into data, feeding recommendation engines and targeted advertising[^terranova]. What is visible is shaped not only by users but also by opaque platform logics.

Due to the significant impact of new media on society, invisibility often links to marginalization, exclusion, and powerlessness. Those who remain unseen may struggle to participate fully in social, political, and economic life. Many organizations strive to ensure marginalized groups' presence in the cyberspace, one of which being AbTeC from Concordia[^abtec].

In contemporary media culture, visibility influences identity formation, activism, and social value. Online presence can generate cultural capital, economic opportunity, or political mobilization. Yet visibility also carries risks: harassment, data exploitation, and performative pressure. The paradox of new media visibility lies in its dual nature: it promises empowerment through exposure while embedding users within systems of control. Thus, visibility today is both a resource and a site of struggle, structuring how society perceives, governs, and connects itself in networked space.
