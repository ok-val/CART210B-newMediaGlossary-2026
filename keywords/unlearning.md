## Unlearning by Andrew Korczak

In new media theory the term unlearning is important because it is related to the process of unlearning habits, assumptions shaped by older media systems and dominant technologies. 

The new media theorists do not seem to be formally coining or designing the term unlearning as a theoretical concept and different theorists may adopt it in different ways.

McCluhan in “The Medium Is the Message,” points out that there is need to unlearn the habit of content instead of form or that one must unlearn print-based models of perceptions in order to understand electric media (radio, television, telegraph).[^MM_und] Therefore, unlearning includes referring to a new way of perceiving the environment (page 2-3). Lev Manovich in “Database as Symbolic Form” indicates that there is need to unlearn cinema-centered and narrative-centered models in order to understand digital media as database and software.[^LM_dat]

For Zylinska the unlearning may refer to the traditional human-centric notion of photography as she argues that photographs are produced through a mix of human and nonhuman agencies (The Creative Power of Nonhuman Photography, page 132).[^JZ_cre] Unlearning relates to current technologies and media practices because the current digital systems reshape how we think, create and interact. The interfaces don’t simply teach us new skills, they also require us to shed old habits, frameworks, in order to function within computational systems. (The Interface Effect, Alexander Gallowoy). In order to engage critically with them there is need to undo habits developed by earlier media and often by platform culture itself. Unlearning includes recognizing what is “natural” or algorithmically shaped. It can also be seen as a necessary tool in resisting manipulation. 

Unlearning is therefore necessary in contemporary new media. 

We must unlearn the idea that seeing is a neutral act.
