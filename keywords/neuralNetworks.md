## Neural Networks by Weini Wang

Neural networks in artificial intelligence teach machines to process data in ways inspired by the human brain. They are a form of machine learning, particularly deep learning, that use layers of interconnected “neurons” to model how humans recognize patterns and make decisions. This idea was proposed by Warren McCulloch and Walter Pitts in 1943, when they attempted to use mathematical models to simulate how information is transmitted between biological neurons. They demonstrated that such networks could continuously learn and adjust themselves in order to improve their understanding of complex data.

In the context of new media, neural networks are significant because they enable the automation of cultural production, personalization, and interpretation at massive scales. Many platforms—such as social media feeds, recommendation systems, and generative-AI tools—rely on deep-learning technologies based on neural networks to shape how users encounter information, aesthetics, and even each other.[^LeCun] Neural networks achieved a breakthrough in the 1980s with the development of the "backpropagation" algorithm, which enabled machines to learn effectively through error feedback.[^Rumelhart] 

For example, neural networks are used in social media platforms for image classification and facial recognition, enabling systems to automatically identify visual patterns and tag content [^googleCloud]. In voice assistants and natural language processing systems, neural networks support the analysis of speech and text, allowing machines to recognize language, interpret meaning, and respond interactively [^IBM]. In addition, recommendation systems used by streaming and media platforms rely on neural-network models to analyze user behavior and personalize content delivery, thereby shaping contemporary media consumption.[^NVIDIA] The MovieLens project further demonstrates how neural-network-based recommendation reshapes media consumption by predicting user preferences.[^MovieLens]

Neural networks therefore function not only as technical infrastructures but also as cultural agents. They reshape how meaning is produced, how attention is distributed, and how creativity is defined in computational environments.
