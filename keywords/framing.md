## Framing

Johanna Drucker’s frame analysis[^1] claims that framing serves to control how we think. Framing chooses what we, the users, see. It isolates whatever the system in question does not want us to see, what happens underneath the hood like what counts as data (like screentime for instance) and what counts as noise (like the action of scrolling). But the meaning behind framing doesn’t come from the frames themselves but how these frames interact with each other. Take virtual machines for example, an operating system nested inside of your own operating system, a perfect example of two frames interacting with each other by embedment. From it, the user will experience security much differently than by only operating on their original operating system.

Which could be linked to Joanna Zylinska’s analogy of her own photography work[^2] where she explains that photos taken on a camera aren’t just something created solely by humans but rather from the interaction between humans and machines. The relationship between the human eye and the camera lens is essential to each other; the human eye representing one frame and the lens another.

In the context of AI[^3] and framing, AI is the result of many frames brought together whether it's a socio-political, environmental, artistic frame or etc. that resulted in how people will have different opinions on AI. Some will see it as something good to create profit while some others will see it as bad because of its environmental cost.
